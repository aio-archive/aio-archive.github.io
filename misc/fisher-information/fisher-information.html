Fisher Information

#information-theory #statistics

Introduction

Fisher information provides a way to measure the amount of information that a random variable contains about some parameter \(\theta\) (such as the true mean) of the random variable’s assumed probability distribution. # Preliminaries A standard problem in statistical estimation is to determine the parameters of a distribution from a sample of data drawn from that distribution. Let \(X_{1}, \cdots, X_{n}\) be drawn i.i.d. from \(\mathcal{N}(\theta,1)\). Suppose we wish to estimate \(\theta\) from a sample of size \(n\). We guess that the sample mean \(\bar{X}_{n} = \dfrac{1}{n}\sum\limits X_i\) is the best estimate of \(\theta\) and we can indeed show that \(\bar{X}_{n}\) is the minimum MSE unbiased estimator.

Let \(\{f(x;\theta)\}, \theta \in \Theta\) denote an indexed family of densities so that \(f(x;\theta) \geq 0, \int f(x;\theta)dx=1\) for all \(\theta \in \Theta\), where \(\Theta\) is called the parameter set.

[!definition] Estimator An estimator for \(\theta\) for a sample size \(n\) is a function \(T\): \(\mathcal{X}^{n}\rightarrow \Theta\).

An estimator is meant to approximate the value of the parameter, therefore it is desirable to have some idea of the goodness of the approximator. We call the difference \(T-\theta\) the error of the estimator. This error itself is a random variable.

[!definition] Bias The bias of an estimator \(T(X_{1},X_{2},\cdots, X_{n})\) for the parameter \(\theta\) is the expected value of the error of the estimator, i.e. \[\mathbb{E}_{\theta}[T(X_{1}, X_{2},\cdots, X_{n})-\theta]\]

The estimator is said to be unbiased if the bias is 0 for all \(\theta \in \Theta\).

[!definition] Consistency of probability An estimator \(T(X_{1},X_{2},\cdots, X_{n})\) for \(\theta\) is said to be consistent in probability if \(T(X_{1},X_{2},\cdots, X_{n}) \rightarrow \theta\) in probability as \(n\rightarrow \theta\).

While consistency is a desirable asymptotic property, we are also interested in the behavior for small sample size.

[!definition] Dominate An estimator \(T_{1}(X_{1},X_{2},\cdots, X_{n})\) is said to dominate another estimator \(T_{2}(X_{1},X_{2},\cdots, X_n)\) if for all \(\theta\) we have: \[\mathbb{E}[T_{1}(X_{1},X_{2}, \cdots, X_{n})-\theta ]^{2}\leq \mathbb{E}[T_{2}(X_{1},X_{2}, \cdots, X_{n})-\theta ]^{2}\]

This raises a natural question: Is there a best estimator of \(\theta\) that dominates every other estimator ? To answer this, we derive the Cramér-Rao lower bound on the MSE of any estimator.

[!definition] Score The score \(V\) is a random variable defined by: \[V = \dfrac{\partial}{\partial \theta} \log f(X;\theta)\]

The mean value of the score is: \[ \begin{aligned} \mathbb{E}[V] &= \int \dfrac{\partial}{\partial \theta}\log f(x;\theta) f(x;\theta)dx \\ &= \int \dfrac{\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}f(x;\theta)}{f(x;\theta)} f(x;\theta)dx \\ &= \int \dfrac{\partial}{\partial \theta}f(x;\theta)dx \\ &= \dfrac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\int f(x;\theta)dx = 0 \end{aligned} \] therefore \(\mathbb{E}[V]^{2}= var(V)\). The variance of the score has a special significance.

[!definition] Fisher information The Fisher information \(J(\theta)\) is the variance of the score: \[J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\theta}\left[\dfrac{\partial}{\partial \theta}\log f(X;\theta)\right]^2\]

If we consider a sample of \(n\) random variable \(X_{1},X_{2},\cdots, X_{n}\) drawn i.i.d. from \(f(x;\theta)\), we have: \[ f(x_{1},x_{2},\cdots, x_{n;\theta)}= \prod_{i=1}^{n}f(x_{i};\theta) \] and the score function is the sum of the individual score functions: \[ \begin{aligned} V(X_{1}, X_{2}, \cdots, X_{n}) &= \dfrac{\partial}{\partial \theta} \log f(X_{1},X_{2}, \cdots, X_{n}; \theta) \\ &= \sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}\dfrac{\partial}{\partial \theta} \log f(X_{i}; \theta) \\ &= \sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}V(X_{i}) \end{aligned} \] where the \(V(X_{i})\) are i.i.d. with zero mean, therefore the \(n\)-sample Fisher information is: \[ \begin{aligned} J_{n}(\theta) &= \mathbb{E}_{\theta}\left[\dfrac{\partial}{\partial \theta} \log f(X_{1},X_{2},\cdots, X_{n};\theta)\right]^{2} \\ &= \mathbb{E}_{\theta}[V^{2}(X_{1},X_{2},\cdots, X_{n})] \\&= \mathbb{E}_{\theta}\left(\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}V(X_{i})\right)^{2} \\ &= \sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}\mathbb{E}_{\theta}[V^{2}(X_{i})] = nJ(\theta) \end{aligned} \]
